# Education in my hometown
Education in China is calling on us to make a change. While students with so-called 'background' more likely to obtain the top tier of what is called the 'elite' of Chinese education system, ordinary people, especially those from the underdeveloped places, deserve to access much more than they have now.

Anhui is what I call an 'embarrassing' province. Unlike Shanghai, Beijing, Guangdong and Jiangsu, it is not really rich. Compared to our eastern neighbours (Jiangsu, Zhejiang and Shanghai), we actually look like poor guys. Sometimes we are even defined as 'born-to-be thieves' by people from rich regions. Also unlike poorer regions, like Xinjiang and Qinghai, we seem to be living in fortune. And this is the reason why we are not recieving aids as many as these regions. Although things are getting much, much more better these days, I still want to ask: is this what we deserve?

I am not here to complain about inequality. Absolute equity is utterly a distant dream. But by working hard, we can prove that we are not less intelligent than our eastern neighbours. We are just waiting for the opportunities.

In Anhui, there are boys and girls who walk hours to the only internet café in town to watch a recorded lecture; who memorize English words under dim dormitory lights after finishing farm work; who dare to dream of Tsinghua, of MIT, of writing the next line of code that changes something — not because they were told they could, but because no one else would tell them they couldn't. They do not need pity. *They need a stage.*

Artificial Intelligence, the field I have chosen to study, or at least to get some insight of, is often said to be an 'elite discipline', dominated by well-funded labs in Beijing, Hangzhou and Silicon Valley. Yet algorithms do not care where you were born. Given data, guidance and a chance to learn, a student from Ma'anshan or Fuyang can build models as rigorous as anyone from the Ivy League. This is the small fairness technology can offer us — if we are willing to bridge the gap, not wait for it to close on its own. If I could not do this, certainly someone could.

So I will study, and I will return hopefully— not necessarily to Anhui in body, but to the idea that knowledge is not a privilege reserved for the coastal few. That excellence has no postcode. *That we, the so-called 'ordinary people' from the ,embarrassing provinces', deserve not only to consume the fruits of others' breakthroughs, but to stand among those who make them.*

Education in China is calling on us to make a change.

Maybe the change begins with us answering that call.

# The root: dialects, tradition and customs
Anhui embraces a vast combination of different Chinese dialects, ranging from Zhongyuan Mandarin, Jianghuai Mandarin, Gan, Wu and Hui, which are more heard, to Min, Hakka and Shehua, which are lesser used and only appear in a few isolated regions.

These dialects themselves, however, are not isolated. They influence each other. As dialects distribute randomly in the Province, the types of combined dialects, and the degree to which they influence each other, result in the diversity of dialects in Anhui. In my city Chizhou, for example, Gan, Jianghuai Mandarin and Wu can all be heard here.

Similar things happen in the country and around the world. Guangdong, for instance, boasts its nearly-become-an-independent-languange dialect Cantonese. Yet for native people from different parts of Guangzhou, their pronunciation and vocabulary are a little different (that is to say, they speak different types of Cantonese), and Cantonese-speaking populations are not limited in a single Guangdong; Cantonese speakers can be found in Guangxi, Hainan, and Hong Kong and Macao SARs. What is more, within Guangdong, there present even different dialects apart from Cantonese.

China is a big country with 56 peoples. You cannot count on us to all become TV hosts who speak 'standard' Mandarin. I am, indeed, a Mandarin advocate, but this does not necessarily mean I should abandon my dialect, which is unique around the world.

In fact, it is precisely because I speak standard Mandarin that I am able to tell people where I come from. And it is because I still understand my dialect that I remember who I am when I go back home. Language is never just a tool for communication; it is a vessel of memory, a map of identity. When a child in Chizhou says 吃夜饭 (*chī yè fàn*) instead of 吃晚饭 (*chī wǎn fàn*) (meaning 'have supper'), or pronounce the word 家 (home) as *gā* inatead of *jiā*, they are not making a mistake—they are inheriting a rhythm of life that has been passed down for generations. When an old man in Huangshan hums a folk song in Hui dialect, he is not being 'unmodern'; he is keeping alive a melody that exists nowhere else on earth.

The tragedy of modern development is not that we learn new languages, but that we forget the old ones. In the age of artificial intelligence, we train machines to understand hundreds of languages, yet we allow human tongues to fall silent. If an algorithm can recognize Cantonese, Wu, or even Shehua, then surely a society can afford to let its people speak them without shame.

China’s strength has never come from uniformity, but from its vastness—its ability to hold contradictions, differences, and local truths within a single civilization. To speak only one kind of 'correct' language is to flatten a mountain range into a parking lot. To protect dialects is not to resist progress; it is to ensure that when we finally arrive at the future, we do not arrive alone, having forgotten the roads that brought us here.

So yes, let us promote Mandarin. Let us connect.

But let us also leave room for the sounds that do not fit the broadcast standard.

*Because sometimes, the most important things are not said in perfect Mandarin.*

*They are whispered in dialects, in kitchens, in villages, in accents that refuse to disappear.*
